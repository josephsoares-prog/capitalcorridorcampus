Understanding BOMA Standards in Commercial Real Estate

Published March 8, 2026 | Business Guide | 8 min read

When evaluating commercial office space, you encounter terms like "2,500 square feet of rentable area" or "usable square footage." But these measurements are not arbitrary—they're calculated using industry standards that significantly affect the price you pay, your ability to compare properties, and your relationship with your landlord.

BOMA standards (Building Owners and Managers Association) define how commercial space is measured and reported. Understanding these standards is essential for tenants evaluating space, landlords setting rates, and anyone making real estate decisions. This article explains what BOMA standards are, why they matter, and how to use them effectively.

What Is BOMA and Why Does It Matter?

BOMA is the Building Owners and Managers Association—a North American industry organization that represents commercial real estate professionals. One of BOMA's primary functions is establishing measurement standards for commercial buildings.

BOMA standards exist because, without them, every landlord would measure space differently. One building might count elevator shafts and mechanical closets as rentable square footage; another might exclude them. Comparing a 10,000 sq ft space in one building to a 10,000 sq ft space in another would be meaningless—you'd have no way to know if you were actually getting equivalent space.

BOMA standards create a common language. When you see "10,000 sq ft of rentable area (BOMA compliant)," you know that measurement was calculated using industry-standard methodology and is comparable to other BOMA-compliant measurements.

The Key Measurement Categories

Rentable Area (RA)

The total area of a floor that can be leased to tenants. This includes the tenant's usable space plus their proportionate share of common areas (lobbies, hallways, restrooms, elevator shafts, etc.). Rentable area is the measurement used to calculate your monthly rent. A typical office building is 80–85% rentable area and 15–20% non-rentable common space.

Usable Area (UA)

The space within your lease that you can actually use—your office, desk space, meeting rooms, etc. This excludes the building's common areas (lobbies, elevators, restrooms). If you lease 1,000 sq ft of rentable area on a floor where common areas are 20% of the total, your usable area is approximately 800 sq ft.

Load Factor (or Load Percentage)

The ratio of rentable area to usable area, expressed as a percentage. If the load factor is 20%, it means for every 100 sq ft of usable space you occupy, you're paying rent on 120 sq ft (100 + 20% for common areas). This is the key number that translates between what the building reports and what you actually use.

Example: You lease 1,000 sq ft of usable space in a building with a 20% load factor. Your rentable area (and what you pay rent on) is 1,000 × 1.20 = 1,200 sq ft.

Why Load Factor Matters to Tenants

Load factor is where many tenants get misaligned with landlord expectations. Here's why it matters:

It Directly Affects Your Rent

If you're comparing two buildings quoting rent at $20/sq ft per year:

  • Building A (15% load factor): 1,000 usable sq ft = 1,150 rentable sq ft = $23,000/year
  • Building B (25% load factor): 1,000 usable sq ft = 1,250 rentable sq ft = $25,000/year

Building B costs $2,000/year more for equivalent usable space, solely because of load factor. This seems like a subtle difference, but over a 5-year lease, it's $10,000. Load factor transparency is critical to accurate comparison.

It Signals Building Efficiency

Buildings with lower load factors (15–18%) are more efficient—they have less "wasted" common area per tenant square foot. Newer, better-designed buildings typically have lower load factors. Older buildings or buildings with inefficient floor plates typically have higher load factors (22–28%). Load factor is one signal of building quality and efficiency.

It Reveals Hidden Costs

Landlords sometimes quote rental rates but obscure load factor in the fine print. A "great deal" at $18/sq ft is less great if the load factor is 30% (pushing actual cost to $23.40/sq ft) versus a higher quoted rate of $22/sq ft with 15% load factor (actual cost $25.30/sq ft for usable space).

BOMA Compliance and Measurement Accuracy

Not all commercial buildings are BOMA-compliant in how they measure space. Some landlords use older standards or non-standard measurements. This creates risk:

  • Disagreement on square footage: If your lease says "approximately 1,000 sq ft" without specifying BOMA measurement, disputes can arise about whether you're actually getting what you paid for.
  • Inability to compare properties: Non-BOMA measurements make it difficult to accurately compare one property to another.
  • Refinancing and valuation issues: Institutional lenders require BOMA-compliant measurements for property valuation and refinancing.

When leasing commercial space, always require that space be measured and reported using BOMA standards. This protects both you and the landlord by creating clarity.

Always verify that space measurements are BOMA-compliant and that load factors are clearly specified in your lease. This single requirement prevents disputes and ensures accurate comparison across properties.

Common Surprises and How to Avoid Them

Surprise 1: "Exclusive Use" vs. "Shared" Space

Some leases give you exclusive use of certain common areas (your own storage closet, conference room access, etc.). BOMA standards may or may not allocate the full cost of these spaces to you. Clarify what's included in your load factor and what's charged separately.

Surprise 2: Anchor Tenant Spaces

In multi-tenant buildings, the load factor varies by floor and location. Floors with fewer, larger tenants (lower per-tenant load) have better load factors. Floors with many small tenants (higher per-tenant load) have worse load factors. If you're signing for a smaller space, expect higher load factor than the building average.

Surprise 3: Common Area Increases

As a building ages or as tenants move in and out, load factors can shift. A stairwell might be sealed off, increasing load factor for remaining tenants. New corridor amenities might be added. Read your lease carefully regarding how future load factor changes are handled.

Using BOMA Standards Effectively When Evaluating Space

Checklist for evaluating commercial space:

  • Confirm that all measurements are BOMA-compliant (BOMA 2010 Standard is current)
  • Obtain the building's load factor and understand what's included (lobbies, restrooms, hallways, etc.)
  • Calculate actual rentable sq ft for your desired usable space (usable × (1 + load factor))
  • Compare rent quotes on a cost-per-usable-sq-ft basis, accounting for load factor differences
  • Verify that your lease defines space measurements in BOMA terms and clarifies the load factor applicable to your space
  • Understand whether the load factor is fixed for the lease term or can change
  • Ask for a floor plan clearly showing rentable versus usable space boundaries

For Landlords: BOMA Compliance as Competitive Advantage

Buildings with clear, BOMA-compliant measurements and transparent load factors compete more effectively. Professional tenants and institutional investors prefer buildings that document space accurately. If you're a landlord, BOMA compliance signals professionalism and reduces friction in lease negotiations.

Conclusion: Know the Standard, Understand the Math

BOMA standards are not obscure technical details—they're the foundation of accurate commercial real estate comparison and pricing. Understanding rentable area, usable area, and load factor gives you the tools to evaluate space accurately, avoid surprises, and make informed decisions.

When you see a commercial space quoted at "$20/sq ft," ask immediately: "Is that rentable or usable sq ft? What's the load factor?" The answers determine whether you're getting a good deal or paying hidden costs for common area space.
