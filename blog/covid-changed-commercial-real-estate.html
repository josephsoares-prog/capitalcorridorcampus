How COVID Permanently Changed Ottawa-Gatineau's Commercial Real Estate Landscape

How the pandemic restructured commercial real estate in the NCR.

The pandemic didn't just disrupt how we work—it fundamentally restructured the commercial real estate market in Ottawa-Gatineau. Three years later, the landscape hasn't returned to normal. It's settled into something entirely new.

Before 2020, commercial real estate in the National Capital Region operated on predictable patterns: long-term leases, centralized office towers, fixed desk configurations. Government was the dominant tenant, and federal workers showed up to the same desk five days a week. That model is gone. What's replaced it is more complex, more flexible, and infinitely better for independent professionals.

The PSPC Consolidation and Government Downsizing

Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC) manages approximately 6 million square meters of office space across the country. Since 2020, PSPC has been systematically consolidating its footprint, closing satellite offices, and shifting government work to hybrid arrangements. The federal government's three-day-in-office mandate—now being rolled out across departments—has formalized this transition.

This consolidation freed up enormous amounts of commercial space. Downtown Gatineau and Hull, which historically depended on steady government tenants, suddenly had vacancy rates climbing. Landlords who'd counted on stable long-term government contracts faced a choice: reconfigure their buildings for a new market, or accept declining occupancy.

For private-sector landlords and innovators, this created an opening. The space was there. The opportunity was there. But the tenant profile needed to shift.

The Hybrid Work Catalyst

Hybrid work didn't just change how employees schedule their week. It changed what professionals need from commercial real estate. A management consultant who used to lease a 500-square-foot office doesn't need that anymore. They need a prestigious address for their business card, meeting room access for client visits, and the credibility that comes from being located in the right place.

The rise of the virtual office model directly addresses this demand. Instead of signing a three-year lease for space they use 40% of the time, professionals can pay $99–$199 monthly for a business address, mail handling, and flexible meeting room access. They get the credibility signal without the overhead.

This shift has accelerated across Canada. Virtual office adoption surged 340% from 2019 to 2023, according to industry data. In government-adjacent markets like the NCR, the adoption is even higher.

The pandemic forced a reckoning in commercial real estate. Buildings that couldn't adapt to hybrid, flexible arrangements faced declining occupancy. Those that leaned into it—offering flexible packages, meeting rooms by the hour, and prestigious business addresses—captured a new market entirely.

The Shift in Demand Patterns

Pre-pandemic, demand clustering in Ottawa-Gatineau was straightforward: major government employers drove occupancy in specific corridors (Parliament Hill, Place du Portage). Private-sector offices filled the gaps.

Post-pandemic, demand is diffuse and flexible. Government workers now split their time between three days in-office and remote work, which means buildings no longer need to accommodate 100% daily occupancy. Instead, they need to be flexible, professional, and able to serve part-time and virtual users.

This creates a new competitive dynamic. A building with a prestigious address, LEED Gold certification, and flexible leasing options (virtual office, hot-desking, part-time, full-time) can serve multiple market segments simultaneously. A building locked into traditional lease structures struggles.
